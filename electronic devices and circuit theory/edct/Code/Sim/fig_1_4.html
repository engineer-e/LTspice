Physics Concepts

Covalent Bonding

Silicon ($\text{Si}$, atomic number 14) has 4 valence electrons in its outer shell. To achieve a stable octet, each atom shares 1 electron with each of its 4 neighboring atoms, forming highly stable covalent bonds.

Thermal Excitation

At absolute zero ($0\text{ K}$), silicon is a perfect insulator. At room temperature ($300\text{ K}$), thermal energy occasionally breaks a covalent bond. The excited electron escapes into the conduction band as a free electron, leaving behind a vacant space called a hole.

Charge Carrier Drift

When an Electric Field (Voltage) is applied, free electrons drift toward the positive terminal (anode). Concurrently, valence electrons can hop into adjacent holes, causing the "hole" (which acts as a positive charge) to drift toward the negative terminal (cathode).

Guide: Covalent Bonding & Semiconductors

1. Visualizing the Covalent Bond (FIG. 1.4)

In the simulator's main window, you will see a $3 \times 3$ grid of Silicon (Si) atoms connected by blue ovals (orbitals). Every overlapping pair represents a single covalent bond where **two valence electrons** are shared.

Notice that every pure Silicon atom sits inside an octet of 8 shared electrons (4 shared pairs), which provides high structural stability.

2. How Conductivity Works visually:

  • Pure Silicon at 0 K: Slide Temperature to $0\text{ K}$. Every electron sits frozen in its bond. Conduction is zero.
  • Thermal Conduction: Increase the temperature slider. Bonds will begin to shake violently. Eventually, some bonds break, launching a glowing **Free Electron** into the background while leaving behind a hollow **Hole (dashed red circle)**.
  • Apply Electric Field: Drag the Electric Field slider. Free electrons instantly drift under force. Notice how valence electrons hop into neighboring vacant holes, causing the holes to drift in the *opposite* direction!

3. Interactive Doping Lab

Doping is the deliberate addition of impurities to alter electrical properties enormously:

  • N-Type (Phosphorus Doped): We replace a Silicon atom with a Pentavalent Phosphorus atom (+5 valence charge). Since it has 5 valence electrons, 4 form covalent bonds, leaving 1 **unbound extra electron** that easily becomes free!
  • P-Type (Boron Doped): We insert a Trivalent Boron atom (+3 valence charge). Since Boron only has 3 valence electrons, it leaves a **permanent bond vacancy (Hole)** in the lattice!
