Physics of the GaAs Covalent Bonding (Fig 1.5)

Gallium Arsenide (GaAs) is a Compound III-V Semiconductor. Unlike elemental Silicon, it consists of alternating Group III (Gallium, Ga) and Group V (Arsenic, As) atoms.

As shown in your diagram, each Gallium has 3 valence electrons, and each Arsenic has 5 valence electrons. Together, they share 8 electrons in covalent bonds to create perfect electronic octets.

Thermal Generation: When you increase temperature, bonds vibrate, transferring energy to electrons. If an electron gains enough energy ($E_g = 1.42\text{ eV}$), it breaks free, leaving an empty spot or Hole.

Why GaAs matters: It has a Direct Bandgap and very high electron mobility, making it extremely efficient for LEDs, lasers, and high-frequency communication circuits compared to Silicon.
